📘 Lecture 1 — Cables, Categories, and Capacity (1 hour)

"Every packet you've ever sent — every TikTok, every Zoom, every login — has, at some point, been a tiny pulse of light or a wiggle of electrons in a copper wire. The Internet is shockingly physical."

1.1 — Network Cables: Copper, Glass, and the Physics In Between

A network cable is a carrier of changes. It transports changes in voltage (copper) or changes in light intensity (fiber). Your NIC wiggles those changes very fast, and the receiver reconstructs the original 1s and 0s.

Two insulated copper wires twisted around each other in pairs, bundled (usually 4 pairs / 8 wires) inside a single jacket terminated with an RJ-45 plug. The twist is the magic: differential signaling cancels noise.

  • UTP (Unshielded) — cheap, flexible, used everywhere.
  • STP (Shielded) — metal foil/braid; for hospitals, factories, near EMI sources.

A single solid copper conductor surrounded by insulation, then a metal mesh shield. Was king in the 1980s (10BASE2, 10BASE5). Today, mostly in cable-Internet drops and some broadcast/telecom links.

A hair-thin strand of ultra-pure glass carrying pulses of laser or LED light.

  • Immune to EMI — lightning, motors, fluorescent lights don't affect it.
  • Fast — modern single-mode fiber does 100 Gbps; lab demos hit 22 Tbps.
  • SMF (Single-Mode) — tiny core (~9 µm), laser, works over kilometers.
  • MMF (Multi-Mode) — fatter core (~50/62.5 µm), cheaper LEDs, works over hundreds of meters.

1.2 — Twisted-Pair Categories at a Glance

99% of the cables you'll touch as a junior admin will be Cat-something. Here's what the "Cat" number actually buys you:

CategoryMax SpeedMax BandwidthMax DistanceTypical UseNotes
Cat 5100 Mbps100 MHz100 mOld offices❌ Avoid for new installs
Cat 5e1 Gbps100 MHz100 mMost homes"e" = enhanced; fine for 1 Gbps
Cat 61 Gbps (10 Gbps ≤ 55 m)250 MHz100 mNew officesCurrent "default"
Cat 6a10 Gbps500 MHz100 mHealthcare, finance"a" = augmented
Cat 710 Gbps600 MHz100 mNicheNot TIA-ratified; often skipped
Cat 825/40 Gbps2000 MHz30 mData-center top-of-rackShort on purpose
🎓 The 100-meter rule. Almost every category caps at 100 m end-to-end (90 m horizontal + two ~5 m patch cables). At higher frequencies, copper signals attenuate and the NIC can't tell 1s from 0s reliably. Cat 8 uses such high frequencies it can only hold the line for 30 m.

🔀 T568A vs T568B Pinouts

The 8 wires inside an RJ-45 plug come in 4 pairs (orange, green, blue, brown). TIA-568 defines two color orderings. They both work as long as both ends use the same one. If one end is A and the other is B, you've made a crossover cable.

1.3 — Speed and Capacity in Plain Language

🚿 The Pipe Analogy

  • Bandwidth = how wide the pipe is (theoretical max). Measured in Hz or bps.
  • Throughput = how much water actually gets through right now. Always ≤ bandwidth.
  • Goodput = useful payload minus headers, retransmissions, and framing overhead.

So your "100 Mbps" Cat 5 cable has 100 Mbps of bandwidth, ~94 Mbps of typical TCP throughput, and ~88 Mbps of goodput. Welcome to the real world.

⚠️ Bits ≠ Bytes

Network speeds use bits (lowercase b). File sizes use bytes (uppercase B). 1 byte = 8 bits. A "100 Mbps" connection downloads at ~12.5 MB/s, not 100 MB/s. ISPs love this confusion.

Latency — the other axis

Two networks can both be "1 Gbps," but if one has 5 ms latency (fiber LAN) and the other has 600 ms (satellite), they feel completely different. Bandwidth is the width of the highway; latency is the commute time.

Lab 1.2 — VirtualBox Deep Dive

A snapshot is a frozen, point-in-time copy of a VM's disk + RAM state.

  • Online snapshot (VM running): captures disk + RAM. Heavier.
  • Offline snapshot (VM powered off): disk only. Smaller, ideal for baselines.

💡 Pro tip: Take a snapshot named clean_baseline before every lab. If you destroy your VM, you're 30 seconds from a fresh start.

  • Full clone — independent copy of the entire virtual disk.
  • Linked clone — tiny diff-disk pointing back to parent. Saves disk space.

VirtualBox has 8 attachment modes. Here's the complete reference:

ModeInternetHost↔VMVM↔VMUse Case
NAT✅❌*❌Just get Internet in the VM
NAT Network✅❌✅Lab of VMs + Internet
Bridged✅✅✅VM looks like real PC on LAN
Internal Network❌❌✅Quarantine / malware analysis
Host-Only❌✅✅SSH from host, no Internet
Not Attached❌❌❌Simulate unplugged cable

*NAT allows host access via port forwarding only.

📘 Lecture 2 — Standards, Bodies & the OSI vs TCP/IP vs Hybrid Debate (1 hour)

"Standards are why your laptop, made by a Taiwanese company, can talk to a Cisco router built in California, over a fiber drawn in Japan, to a server running open-source software written by a Finn — and none of those people ever met."

2.1 — Why Standards Exist (and who writes them)

Imagine the early 1970s. Every computer manufacturer built its own networking protocol. None of them talked to each other. The networking world was a Tower of Babel.

Then came TCP/IP (ARPANET → Internet) and the OSI model. Today, we use TCP/IP in practice and OSI as the teaching framework.

2.2 — The OSI 7-Layer Model

In 1984, ISO published the OSI Reference Model. OSI never won as a protocol stack, but it completely won as the language we use to talk about networks.

🎓 Mnemonics: Bottom-up: Please Do Not Tell Sales People Anything. Top-down: All People Seem To Need Data Processing.

2.3 — The TCP/IP (Internet / DoD) Model

The TCP/IP model was created before OSI and was the practical scaffolding for the Internet. It has fewer layers because the upper three OSI layers blur into "Application."

TCP/IP LayerRoughly maps to OSIExamples
4. ApplicationOSI 5+6+7HTTP, DNS, SMTP, SSH, TLS
3. TransportOSI 4TCP, UDP
2. InternetOSI 3IP, ICMP, ARP*
1. Network AccessOSI 1+2Ethernet, Wi-Fi, MAC, cables

2.4 — The Hybrid (5-Layer) Model

Most modern textbooks teach a 5-layer hybrid: Physical · Data Link · Network · Transport · Application. This keeps the L1/L2 distinction while not pretending Session and Presentation are "real" layers in practice.
