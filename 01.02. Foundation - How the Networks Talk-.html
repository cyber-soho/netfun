Lecture 1

What Is a Network?

This section breaks down the fundamental definition of a network. By interacting with the elements below, you will understand the three indispensable ingredients required to make digital communication possible.

"A network is two or more devices connected by a medium that lets them exchange data using agreed rules."

Explore the Three Essential Ingredients

💻

1. Devices

Endpoints & Intermediates

Endpoints (make the data): Laptop, phone, server, printer, IoT sensors.

Intermediate Devices (move the data): Switch, router, Access Point (AP), firewall, modem.

🛜

2. Medium

The physical pathway

  • Copper: twisted-pair (Ethernet), coaxial.
  • Fibre optics: pulses of light through glass.
  • Radio waves: Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, cellular.

All three carry the same logical data.

📜

3. Rules (Protocols)

The grammar of the network

How devices take turns talking. Without protocols = digital chaos.

Devices speak dozens of protocols simultaneously, each doing one job (addressing, error-checking).

Standardised by IETF, IEEE, ISO.

🗝️

Key Takeaway

A network = devices + medium + rules. Strip any one of those three and you have no network — just hardware in a box.

Lecture 1

Network Types & Scales

This section explores how networks are categorized by their geographic reach. Discover how smaller networks act like Russian nesting dolls, plugging into larger ones to form the global internet.

🎧 PAN

~1 meter

Personal Area Network. Connecting immediate personal devices. Example: Phone ↔ earbuds (Bluetooth).

🏢 LAN

Building

Local Area Network. The foundation of businesses and homes. Example: Home or office Wi-Fi & Ethernet.

🏙️ MAN

City

Metropolitan Area Network. Bridging multiple buildings. Example: University Campus, ISP municipal fibre.

🌍 WAN

Globe

Wide Area Network. Spanning countries and continents. Example: The internet itself.

How They Nest

Smaller networks plug into larger ones.

Function-based Flavours

You will also hear terms based on purpose rather than size:

Lecture 1

Speed & Capacity

This section clarifies the critical difference between theoretical capacity and actual user experience. Use the interactive chart to visualize the "Highway Analogy" and learn how to estimate real-world download times.

The Highway Analogy

Network Term Highway Equivalent
Bandwidth Number of lanes (Theoretical max)
Throughput Cars actually arriving per minute (Actual data)
Latency Travel time from on-ramp to exit (Delay)
Jitter Variation in arrival times
Packet Loss Cars that crashed and never arrived

Bandwidth vs Throughput (1 Gbps Link)

Due to protocol overhead and potential packet loss, Throughput is always lower than Bandwidth.

Units of Capacity

Bits vs Bytes — read the lowercase 'b'

8 bits = 1 Byte

  • bps bits per second (base unit)
  • Kbps kilobits/s (10³)
  • Mbps megabits/s (10⁶)
  • Gbps gigabits/s (10⁹) - Modern Standard
  • Tbps terabits/s (10¹²)

Speed Sanity Formula

Estimate Download Time

Time(s) ≈ File(Mb) ÷ Throughput(Mbps)

Quick Mental Check:

  • 1 GB file = 8000 Mb
  • On 100 Mbps connection → 80s ideal
  • Reality (~50% overhead) → expect 2 minutes

Lecture 2

Getting Online

This section details the mechanical requirements for a device to communicate on a network. Use the interactive tabs to explore the "Four Pieces" every device needs, and step through the automated DHCP handshake process.

Four Pieces Every Device Needs

Miss any one of these, and your users will complain: "I have Wi-Fi but no internet."

📍

The Postal Address

Its identity on the network. Without it, data doesn't know where to go.

IPv4 (Legacy but common) 192.168.1.10

32 bits, ~4.3 billion total.

IPv6 (Future-proof) 2001:db8::1

128 bits, ~3.4 × 10³⁸ total.

🏘️

The Neighbourhood Boundary

Tells your device which IP addresses are local (talk directly) and which are remote (send via gateway).

  • 255.255.255.0 (/24) 254 usable hosts
  • 255.255.0.0 (/16) 65 534 hosts
🚪

The Front Door

The router's IP address on your subnet. Anything destined off-LAN travels through it.

Warning: Without a gateway, the device is island-locked. It can print to the local printer, but cannot reach Google.
📖

The Internet Phonebook

Translates human names (google.com) into machine IPs (142.250.190.46).

How Devices Get the 4 Pieces

Static

Set by Human, manually

Servers, Printers

DHCP (Dynamic)

Set by Router, automatically

Laptops, Phones

Modern home/SoHo = DHCP for clients, static for infra.

The DHCP Handshake D.O.R.A.

D

Discover Client → Broadcast

"Anyone there? I need an IP."

O

Offer Server → Client

"Here, take 192.168.1.42."

R

Request Client → Broadcast

"Yes, I'll take it."

A

Acknowledge Server → Client

"Confirmed — it's yours for 24h."

Lecture 2

Documentation

This section covers the "Boring Superpower" of network engineering. Proper documentation separates juniors from seniors. Review the 5 essential artifacts required for every build.

The Golden Rule

"No diagram = no network. If it's not drawn and named, it does not exist on Monday morning when somebody calls in sick."

Logical vs Physical

Logical Diagram

Subnets, VLANs, routing. Audience: Engineers. "What talks to what". Stable across physical moves.

Physical Diagram

Cables, ports, racks. Audience: Technicians. "What plugs into what". Changes on every move.

Naming Conventions

Consistency > cleverness. Future-you needs to grep this.

  • Devices: SITE-ROLE-NUMBER → MTL-SW-01
  • VLANs: purpose-based → VLAN10_CORP
  • Files: dated → topology_v1_2026-04-28.drawio

The Minimum Viable Network Doc Pack

1. Logical
VLANs, IPs, Routes
🔌
2. Physical
Racks, Cables
📋
3. Inventory
Model, MAC, Location
🔢
4. IP Plan
Subnet allocation
⏱️
5. Change Log
Every mod, dated

Skip any of these and future-you will hate present-you.
